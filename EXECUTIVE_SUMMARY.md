## Key Findings

### 1. Data Retention Policy Violated

OCSO publicly claims data is deleted every 30 days. Audit evidence shows 463 searches accessed data up to 524 days oldâ€”routine searches for "patrol" and traffic violations accessed year-old data.

### 2. Nationwide Surveillance Capability

Nearly half (46.8%) of searches queried multiple networks. The average multi-network search accessed 1,800 networks; the largest accessed 6,709 networks and 100,034 cameras simultaneously.

### 3. Accountability Blocked

100% redaction of officer names and case numbers makes it impossible to verify whether searches served legitimate purposes. The "audit" capability advertised to the public is meaningless.

### 4. Contract Favors Vendor

The Flock Safety contract limits liability to 12 months of fees, lacks breach notification requirements, and grants Flock perpetual rights to use "anonymized" data for machine learning.

## Implications

### For Citizens

- License plates function as permanent tracking beacons
- Travel history retained far beyond stated policy
- No way to know if you've been searched or why

### For Policy

- Stated policies do not match operational reality
- Oversight mechanisms are ineffective
- Multi-agency networks bypass local control

### For Accountability

- Redacted audits prevent verification
- No independent oversight exists
- Vendor contract lacks accountability provisions

---

## Recommendations

1. **Enforce Retention Limits** - Technical controls preventing access beyond stated retention period
2. **Require Justification** - Block searches without documented criminal justice purpose
3. **Enable Real Audits** - Disclose officer/case information for accountability
4. **Limit Network Scope** - Require warrant or supervisor approval for multi-network searches
5. **Renegotiate Contract** - Add breach notification, audit rights, and meaningful liability
